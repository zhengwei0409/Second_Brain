# Tea

## 1. One plant, many teas

**Definition:** Green tea, oolong, black tea, and pu'er all come from the leaves of the exact same plant, Camellia sinensis.

**Why it matters:** Once you get this, the whole confusing world of tea names stops being a list of random plants and becomes one plant processed in different ways — same starting point, different paths.

**Question:** If someone hands you a green tea and a black tea, can you explain why they taste so different even though they grew on the same kind of tree?

**Answer:** They taste different because of how the leaves were processed after picking, not because they came from different plants. The green tea was treated with heat right away to stop oxidation, keeping it light and fresh-tasting. The black tea was left to oxidize fully, which is a browning reaction (like a cut apple turning brown in the air) that deepens the color and creates a stronger, bolder flavor. Same leaf, different treatment, very different result.

---

## 2. Oxidation is the dial that creates green, oolong, and black tea

**Definition:** Oxidation is what happens when a cut leaf is exposed to air and starts to browning, kind of like a sliced apple turning brown on the counter.

**Why it matters:** Tea makers control how long they let this happen, and that single choice is what separates green tea (no oxidation) from oolong (some oxidation) from black tea (full oxidation).

**Question:** Can you put green tea, oolong, and black tea in order from least to most oxidized, and explain what changes about the leaf as oxidation increases?

**Answer:** Order: green tea (0%) → oolong (roughly 10%–80%) → black tea (close to 100%). As oxidation increases, the leaf color shifts from green to brown to almost black, and the flavor shifts from fresh and grassy, to floral or roasted (depending on how much oxidation), to bold and malty. More oxidation also generally means a stronger, deeper flavor and less of that "fresh leaf" taste.

## 3. Oolong isn't one fixed thing, it's a wide range

**Definition:** Oolong covers a huge stretch of oxidation, roughly 10% to 80%, so names like Tieguanyin or Da Hong Pao describe a traditional style and region, not an exact oxidation number.

**Why it matters:** This explains why oolong teas can taste wildly different from each other, light and floral on one end, dark and roasted on the other, even though they're all called oolong.

**Question:** Why can't you point to one specific percentage and say "that's what makes something an oolong"?

**Answer:** Because oolong isn't defined by hitting an exact oxidation number, it's defined by a traditional production method tied to a specific region and style, passed down over time. The oxidation level is just a rough side effect of that traditional process, and it varies a lot, sometimes even within the same named tea (like Tieguanyin, which has a lighter "fragrant" version and a darker "roasted" version). So the name tells you about heritage and technique, not a fixed chemistry number.

## 4. Pu'er plays a different game: fermentation, not oxidation

**Definition:** Pu'er is fermented by microbes over time, similar to how cheese or wine develop, instead of being oxidized like the other teas.

**Why it matters:** This is why pu'er can actually improve with age for years or decades, while most other teas are best fresh, it's a completely different aging logic.

**Question:** Why does it make sense that a pu'er tea cake would have a year printed on the package, but you'd never see that on a bag of green tea?

**Answer:** Because pu'er keeps changing after it's made. Microbes continue breaking it down slowly over time, which mellows out the flavor and makes older pu'er more prized and valuable, similar to aged wine or cheese. Green tea, on the other hand, is "finished" once it's processed, oxidation is stopped on purpose, and from that point on it can only get worse (going stale), never better. So the year matters for pu'er because age is part of the product's value, while for green tea, age is just a countdown to staleness.

## 5. Herbal "teas" from the kopitiam aren't real tea at all

**Definition:** Drinks like chrysanthemum tea or herbal cooling tea (凉茶) are made by boiling various herbs and roots, not by using leaves from the tea plant.

**Why it matters:** This stops you from lumping them in with Tieguanyin or pu'er, they belong to a totally separate category (traditional Chinese medicine-style herbal drinks), grouped by what health effect they're supposed to have rather than by oxidation or fermentation.

**Question:** If a friend says "chrysanthemum tea is a type of green tea," what's wrong with that statement, and how would you correct it?

**Answer:** The statement mixes up two completely different categories. Chrysanthemum tea isn't made from Camellia sinensis (the tea plant) at all, it's made from boiling chrysanthemum flowers, the same way other kopitiam herbal drinks are made from things like sugarcane or mixed herbs. It's not "a type of" green tea, oolong, or any real tea, it's a separate category of herbal drink, traditionally grouped by its supposed health effect (like cooling the body down) rather than by oxidation level the way real teas are.
